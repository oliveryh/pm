## Highlights

- You should expect people to all be [managers of one](https://blog.boleary.dev/gitlabs-values/#managers-of-one) - responsible for their time and actions in getting the job done that needs doing and that *you hired them to do*. Couple this with a [bias for action over inaction](https://blog.boleary.dev/gitlabs-values/#bias-for-action) and [measuring results and not hours worked](https://blog.boleary.dev/gitlabs-values/#measure-results-not-hours), and you have a recipe for success. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01gm5fm2ddxpjqagvwqhqmb93k))

- One of the key concepts to discuss here is that [everything is in draft](https://blog.boleary.dev/gitlabs-values/#everything-is-in-draft). As often as possible, you should ship something rather than wait. At GitLab, one way we talk about this is the concept of “MVC” or [minimal viable change](https://blog.boleary.dev/gitlabs-values/#move-fast-by-shipping-the-minimal-viable-change). Put out of your mind the minimally viable product or even the minimally viable feature. Those things are too large and complex to reason with – that's why we end up with all the jokes around estimation and shipping late in software. You can, however, reason about a minimal change—and if it makes the product better (even if you know the real feature needs a lot of work) then SHIP IT. Shipping [early](https://blog.boleary.dev/gitlabs-values/#dont-wait), [frequently](https://blog.boleary.dev/gitlabs-values/#reduce-cycle-time), and [on a cadence](https://blog.boleary.dev/gitlabs-values/#set-a-due-date) allows you to shorten the time to feedback and accelerate getting you to where you really need to be.

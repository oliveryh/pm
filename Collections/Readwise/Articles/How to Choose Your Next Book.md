## Highlights
- **Build your foundation.** The key here is brutal honesty with yourself about [what you really know](https://fs.blog/2013/12/circle-of-competence/). Take the time to do a [Feynman One Pager](https://fs.blog/2012/04/learn-anything-faster-with-the-feynman-technique/) on an idea you think you know really well. While easy, this process will reveal any gaps you have in your knowledge. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01gtvek88j6nmk0jrddzqhx1pa))
- **The multidisciplinary mind understands the basic ideas.** Acquiring [the basic mental models from multiple disciplines](https://www.fs.blog/mental-models/) allows you to see things that other people can’t. You don’t need to understand the latest study in biology, but you sure as heck better understand the concept of evolution because it applies to so much more than animals. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01gtvem9w3g2jk6b12b4505th0))
- Lindy Effect ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01gtvew2b237ze38w9kvxf072j))
- If you were to look at a typical person’s reading list, the vast majority of books would be crammed into the recent, low-value portion of the curve while many fewer books would occupy the much larger high-value, older section of the curve.
  So your ROI on reading and understanding a concept from 500 years ago is highly likely to be exponentially greater in the long run than one presented only 5 years ago.
  What I’m trying to get at is that the more fundamental or closer to the source that you move, the better the ROI in the long run. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01gtvetn2g2hfn61aw7jah9kma))

## Focused versus Diffuse Thinking

- There are two different types of thinking:
    - Diffuse Mode - Look at things broadly
    - Focused Mode - Look at things in a predetermined and practised method
- Can't be in both thinking modes at the same time
- When learning something new, you need to be able to move between the diffuse and focused modes easily
- Metaphors provide powerful techniques for learning

### What is Learning

- The brain has many different resting states that have particular purposes
- When we learn new things, synapses are formed on the dendrite

## Procrastination, Memory, and Sleep

### Procrastination

- Process of Procrastination
    1. You observe an unhappy feeling and the **insular cortex (pain) is activated**
    2. You then revert your attention to something more pleasant
    3. You are temporarily happy

### Memory and Chunking

There are two types of memory

- Long Term Memory - Memory for long term stored in many different regions of the brain
    - Spaced Repetition is required to reinforce ideas that are stored into long-term memory
- Working Memory - Memory for immediate and conscious mind located in the **prefrontal cortex**
    - Can **hold around four chunks** of working memory

### Sleep

- Being awake creates metabolic toxic products in the brain. When we sleep your brain cells shrink, allowing these toxins to be flushed out.

## Chunking - The Essentials

### What is a chunk?

- Memorizing a fact without understanding or context doesn't help you understand what you're learning
- Rote structured repetition (Flashcards) alongside diffuse mode freestyle methods (Exploration of those concepts) work well together
- A chunk represents a well developed neural pathway

### How to Form a Chunk

- Understand the pattern you want to master for yourself (e.g. chords from a song when learning the guitar)

Process for chunking

1. Devote all your attention to the process of chunking
2. Understand the basic idea you're trying to chunk. Often understanding an idea fully is when you're able to perform something yourself as opposed to just observing it.
3. Gain context, understanding how or when to use this chunk.
4. Gain a top down view, seeing how ideas fit together and sit next to other parallel ideas.

### Illusions of Competence

- Rereading a piece of text has been shown to not be very effective (Karpicke)
- Recall (seeing what you can remember from the text) has been shown to be better for retention
- Once should understand the chunks of the problem before doing concept mapping
- Glancing at a solution and thinking you would have known that yourself is not an effective form of learning as you haven't formed the neural connections yourself
- Highlighting and underlining must be done very carefully and kept to a minimum, otherwise this practice is not useful

## Seeing the Bigger Picture

### What Motivates You?

- Neuromodulators are chemicals that influence how a neuron responds to other neurons.
- There are 3 key types of Diffuse Neuromodulators:
    1. Acetylcholine
        - Form connections for focused learning
    2. Dopamine
        - Released when we receive an unexpected rewards. Predicts not only current reward but also future reword.
        - Loss of dopamine neurons leads to Parkinson's.
    3. Serotonin
        - Prozac (prescribed for clinical depression) raises the level of serotonin activity
        - Low levels of serotonin correlate to high risk activity
- Amygdala is part of the limbic system, associated with learning.

### The Value of a Library of Chunks

- Experts use extended periods of time reading to hold many chunks at the same time
- Transfer - Allows you to take what you've learned in one subject area and apply it to another
- Solving Problems
    - Sequential Learning
    - Holistic Learning - Most problem solving requires this type of learning as a more nuanced style is required
- Finding the common underlying concepts of many different subject areas is a good way to grasp many concepts at the same time.

### Overlearning, Choking, Einstellung, and Interleaving

- Repeating something you know well can give you an illusion of competence
- We should deliberately focus on what we find difficult, using different techniques, allowing us to understand there are different ways to approach a problem
- Einstellung - Refers to a person's predisposition to solve a given problem in a specific manner even though better or more appropriate methods of solving the problem exist..
- Interleaving - Combing your knowledge of solving a problem with different techniques, problem types etc. This strengthens a particular problem solving method with different problem sets.
- Practice and repetition (flashcards) is important in building solid neural patterns and interleaving promotes flexibility and creativity (unseen problems).

## Procrastination

- Procrastination is common but will power is not, and cannot be easily forced.
- We procrastinate when we feel uneasy about completing a particular task, doing something in the short term to feel better (go on social media), in order to feel better again.
- Chunking leads to habits, which in turn allow us to perform tasks with a reduced amount of energy
- Habits
    - Cue
    - Routine
    - Reward - Every habit develops and continues because there is some sort of reward
    - Belief - Habits work because we have belief

### Process vs Product

- To prevent procrastination you should focus on the process instead of product (doing a pomodoro instead of expecting to finish the question)
- Focusing on the process instills a habit, allowing us to avoid procrastination and avoid judging our abilities to finish a task.

### Reducing Procrastination

- In order to stop procrastination, we should:
    1. Reduce the cues for our bad habits. Cues can be some of the following (place, time, feeling, reaction).
    2. Change the routines associated to a cue. For example a craving for something sweet could be replaced with the routine of eating an apple, rather than a chocolate biscuit.
    3. Add rewards to your work.

### The Value of Teamwork

- Working in groups can extend your diffuse mode to be even more broad
- Explaining concepts to others will also reinforce your own learning

### Test Checklist

- Did you make a serious effort to understand the text? Just hunting for relevant worked-out examples doesn't count.
- Did you work with classmates on homework problems or at least check your solutions with others?
- Did you attempt to outline every homework problem solution before working with classmates? Did you participate actively in homework group discussions, contributing ideas and asking questions?
- Did you consult with the instructor or teaching assistants when you were having trouble with something?
- Did you understand all your homework problem solutions when they were handed in?
- Did you ask in class for explanations of homework problem solutions that weren't clear to you?
- If you had a study guide, did you carefully go through it before the test and convince yourself you could do everything on it?
- Did you attempt to outline lots of problem solutions quickly without spending time on the algebra and calculations?
- Did you go for the study guide and problems with classmates and quiz one another?
- If there was a review session before the test, did you attended and asked questions about anything you weren't sure about?
- Did you get a reasonable night's sleep before the test?

### Hard Start - Jump to Easy

- Start with the hardest problems but be prepared to quickly jump to the easiest problems should you feel like the problem isn't going well
- This will kick start your diffuse mode of thinking

# General Notes

- Chunking (know how smaller concepts fits in the bigger concept, like puzzle pieces)
- Illusion of competence (taking brief notes avoid this)
- Recall (after reading summarize in a few minutes your takeaway)
- Bite-sized tests (auto-test yourself about new material)
- Overlearning (avoid marathon sessions, spread learning over many sessions in different learning modes)
- Process over product (aim for time goals)
- Interleaving (know how but also know when to apply a concept, context is important)
- Methapors and analogies (use them when teaching/sharing what you’ve learned)
- Study groups (share and get different points of view, learning in groups adds to the experience)

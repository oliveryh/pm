# Focus on the Now

> Just in time is better than just in case. Think about all the knowledge you've acquired, photos you've taken. They should be ready just in time, not accumulated for every conceivable place you might end up

To build on this, it's more effective if you immerse yourself in topics that are directly related to your current projects. Researching something you're not actively working on will create unnecessary context switching.

# Fastest Ways to Learn

By far the biggest beneficiary of you trying to help past you is future you. If others benefit, that's icing on the cake.

- Write blogs and tutorials and cheatsheets.
- Speak at meetups and conferences.
- Ask and answer things on StackOverflow or Reddit.
- Avoid the walled gardens like Slack and Discord, they're not public.
- Enjoyed a coding video? Reach out to the speaker/instructor and thank them, and ask questions.
- Make PR's to libraries you use. [^1]

# Process of Learning

- Recognise patterns in how the underlying process it repeated across different applications
- Form novel ideas from this though process [^2]

# Expiring vs Permanent Skills [^collab]

In computer science for example, the field is moving fast and so it's hard to know what to learn. Splitting the potential topics into the following helps:
- **Expiring Skills**: Skills that are vital at a given time but are prone to diminishing as a field evolves
- **Permanent Skills**: As essential a hundred years ago as they are today

This helps pick out difference between learning a new Javascript framework, versus understanding how HTTP works. One has been around for decades, while the other may be outdated in a year or so.

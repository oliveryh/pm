# Methodology

1. List every concept/object/process I spend time/energy/money on
2. Assess value, urgency, and cost
3. Remove low-value or high-cost items; make time for what matters

- **Document** — map inputs (digital in-tray), actions (internet habits), and outputs (created/archived work)
- **Reduce** — cut the number of inputs; uninstall apps, unsubscribe from feeds
- **Consolidate** — funnel what remains into a single stream with minimal distractions
- **Understand motivations** — avoid maintaining systems for their own sake
- **Maintain** — do regular check-ins to keep the system honest

Some examples of inefficiencies include:

- Food shopping multiple times per week
- Going through emails
- Checking my monthly budget
- Reading news websites (BBC, Hacker News)
- Writing To Do lists which are most likely repeats of information that I have stored elsewhere
- Reading about something that has no clear influence on my life at all
- Doing chores late on a Sunday instead of winding down and relaxing
- Watching TV in general

# Build Tools for Workflows

https://thesephist.com/posts/tools/

Tools we consume will largely fall short of what we expect. When others others build tools for us to use, they either design tools after their own workflows and mental models, or design for a mass market.

Instead of trying to fit our workflows to fit around the tools available to us, we should be designing tools to fit around our workflows.

Not only this, but the investment in time is worth it. I was able to build a task app that fixed my failed attempts of trying to fit my workflow around mass marketed tools.

I'm not able to consolidate all the apps I once used, into tools that adapt to my workflow. The apps that I build, along with the highly customizable tools (Notion & Obsidian).

The other benefit of writing your own tools is ownership and longevity. Obsidian is stored in Markdown, a format that will be supported for years to come.
